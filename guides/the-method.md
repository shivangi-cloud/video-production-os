# The Method: Turning a Client's Numbers Into a Film

The system behind a 60 second corporate results film that doesn't look like a PowerPoint.

## The two sentences that run every edit
1. Fast enough to feel successful. Slow enough to prove it.
2. Every pause must be caused by a result.

The film sprints through proof (events, stores, crowds), slams to a stop, a number lands and holds, energy releases again. The stop itself is the applause cue.

## How a number lands (the physics)
- Riser into the stop, two frames of black silence, sub bass hit as the number lands.
- The hero achievement number visibly breaks past the 100 mark, overshoots slightly, locks.
- Hold every important number at least 1.2 seconds, over footage dimmed to ~35%.
- Write +53% for growth, plain 117% for achievement. Explain units once, small, never repeat.
- Three numbers maximum on any frame. Ever.

## Numbers inside the world (not text cards)
The premium versions: a number as neon on the venue wall, a pour writing it in liquid,
foil embossing like a label, a count chalked along a real shelf edge, light through amber glass.
Type on black is still a slide, however good the font.

## The "international" look is restraint, not budget
One warm grade on everything. One grotesk typeface, ivory only, two sizes per frame max.
Crop into details, never show a full badly lit shop. Vertical phone footage becomes
three-panel triptychs. Cut on the beat, ramp on physical actions. Hard ending, no fade.

## Rescuing weak client photos
Weak photos get 12-15 frames of screen time inside fast sections. The best few get parallax.
Products get cut out and rebuilt on brand color fields. Grids absorb mediocrity:
eight average store photos tiling on fast read as "we are everywhere."

## Two films, two jobs
The results film performs. The speech backdrop serves: slow, high headline, empty center,
frozen final frame delivered as a 4K PNG for the venue to display.
